Ultrapure Water & Surfaces: Why 18.2 MΩ·cm Never Lasts

At 25 °C, “pure” water limited only by its own self-ionization reaches a resistivity of 18.2 MΩ·cm (conductivity 0.055 µS/cm). That’s the gold standard of Milli-Q systems and ASTM Type I water.

But the moment this water meets air, it absorbs CO₂, dissolves O₂, and accumulates trace organics from surfaces. The result? Resistivity collapses to ~1 MΩ·cm, electrodes behave strangely, and every “clean” experiment becomes vulnerable to contamination.

⚡ Quick facts

  • CO₂: crashes resistivity from 18.2 → ~1 MΩ·cm (pH ~5.6 at 420 ppm CO₂).
  • O₂: barely changes conductivity, but ruins electrochemistry (ORR currents).
  • Hydrocarbons: not conductive, but form nm-thin films that block Au/Pt active sites.
  • Cleanrooms: control particles, not CO₂/organics; gloveboxes remove O₂/H₂O but often not CO₂/HCs.

🧪 Why it matters

This is why labs bubble Ar/N₂ through ultrapure water, or blanket it with inert gas: not to make it more “pure” than 18.2 MΩ·cm, but simply to stop CO₂ from knocking it down to 1 MΩ·cm. And for surfaces, solvent rinses alone are never enough—UV–ozone, O₂ plasma, or flame annealing are required to really strip hydrocarbons.

📖 Technical Appendix

  • Charge balance: [H⁺] = Kw/[H⁺] + K₁[CO₂*]/[H⁺] + 2K₁K₂[CO₂*]/[H⁺]².
  • [CO₂*] = K_H pCO₂; with KH ≈ 3.3×10⁻² mol·L⁻¹·atm⁻¹.
  • Conductivity κ = Σ λᵢ°[i] / 1000. Resistivity ρ = 1/κ.
  • Constants at 25 °C: Kw = 10⁻¹⁴; pKₐ₁=6.35, pKₐ₂=10.33; λ°(H⁺)=349.6, OH⁻=198.5, HCO₃⁻=44.5, CO₃²⁻=138 S·cm²·mol⁻¹.
